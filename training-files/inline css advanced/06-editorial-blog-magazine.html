The Long Twilight of Liberal Democracy

Three decades after Fukuyama declared history over, scholars are confronting a more uncomfortable truth: the institutions we built to guarantee freedom may be the very things dismantling it.

ER
Eleanor Rashid
April 12, 2025 · 24 min read

In 1992, Francis Fukuyama published The End of History and the Last Man, arguing that liberal democratic capitalism had triumphed as humanity's final form of government. The book was wrong in ways he never anticipated — not because liberal democracy failed to spread, but because the mechanisms of that spread contained, embedded within them, the seeds of institutional decay.

This is not a story about authoritarianism winning. It is something more vertiginous: a story about democracy undermining itself through the very tools designed to guarantee it. Electoral systems, judicial review, freedom of the press, civil society — each of these was meant to act as a bulwark. In the twenty-first century, each is functioning as a lever.

The Accountability Paradox

Political scientists call it the accountability paradox: the more transparent a government becomes, the more opportunities emerge for bad-faith actors to exploit that transparency. Freedom of information requests, once celebrated as tools of investigative journalism, now serve as harassment vectors against civil servants. Campaign finance disclosures, designed to surface corruption, are now used to intimidate donors to unpopular causes.

"The institutions we built to guarantee freedom may be the very things dismantling it — not through failure, but through a kind of terrible success."

— Eleanor Rashid

What makes this erosion particularly difficult to address is that it rarely looks like erosion in the moment. Each individual action — a lawsuit, an injunction, a parliamentary procedure — is technically legitimate. The problem is structural, emergent, the product of thousands of legal moves that individually comply with the rules while collectively dismantling the purpose those rules were designed to serve.

The Speed Problem

Democratic institutions were designed for a different temporal rhythm. Courts take years. Legislatures take months. Investigative journalism takes weeks. The information environment, however, now moves in hours — sometimes minutes. By the time a judicial review has concluded, the norm being challenged has already been violated thousands of times, and the violation has been normalised through sheer repetition.
